Equipment & Gear Deductions
---------------------------

Deductions for photography equipment and related gear

Camera & Equipment Purchases

You can deduct the full cost of cameras, lenses, and computers using Section 179 or depreciation. Connecticut conforms to federal rules, so assets may be written off immediately. Track usage and use IRS Form 4562 to report.

$

Small Equipment & De Minimis Expensing

Items under $2,500 like tripods, accessories, and SD cards can be expensed using the IRS safe harbor rule. Connecticut follows federal treatment—no special state adjustment needed. Keep receipts and note business use clearly.

$

Sales & Use Tax on Equipment

Sales tax paid on in-state purchases becomes part of your equipment's cost basis. If you buy out-of-state without paying sales tax, Connecticut use tax is due. File and remit use tax on your CT return. Keep purchase records.

$

Repair & Maintenance Costs

Camera repairs, lens calibrations, and sensor cleanings are deductible operating expenses. Connecticut allows full deduction for such maintenance costs. If services were taxed, the sales tax is part of the expense. Save invoices.

$

Software & Digital Tools

Image editing software, storage subscriptions, and apps used for photography are deductible. Connecticut conforms to federal treatment. Electronically delivered software is exempt from CT sales tax; physical media may be taxed.

$

Studio/Office Rent, Utilities & Insurance in Connecticut
--------------------------------------------------------

Deductions for studio and office expenses

Studio Rent & Utilities

Rent paid for studios, offices, or co-working spaces is fully deductible. Utilities like electricity, water, internet, and heating used for business are also deductible. Connecticut aligns with federal rules—no extra limits or requirements.

$

Home Office Deduction

If a portion of your home is used exclusively for photography work, you can deduct home office expenses. Connecticut accepts both the simplified method and actual expense method from your federal return. Use IRS Form 8829 if itemizing.

$

Business Insurance Premiums

Premiums for business insurance—liability, equipment, property, and E&O—are deductible. Connecticut does not tax insurance premiums and conforms to federal deductibility rules. Keep all policy records and payment documentation.

$

Storage & P.O. Boxes

Fees for off-site storage units, lockers for gear, or business P.O. boxes are deductible. Connecticut follows federal treatment—no disallowance or adjustment. Ensure usage is solely for business and not personal storage.

$

Minor Repairs & Studio Maintenance

Repairs or upkeep to leased studio spaces—like repainting, lighting fixes, or cleaning services—are fully deductible. Connecticut follows federal treatment. Save repair invoices and maintenance receipts for documentation.

Travel & Transportation in Connecticut
--------------------------------------

Deductions for travel and transportation expenses

Business Vehicle Use (Mileage or Actual Costs)

Photographers can deduct car expenses using either the standard mileage rate or actual cost method. For 2024, the IRS rate is $0.67/mile. Connecticut follows federal rules—no separate calculation required. Maintain a mileage log and expense records.

$

Travel for Business (Airfare, Hotels, Meals)

Airfare, lodging, local transit, and baggage fees for client shoots or conferences are deductible if travel is primarily for business. Connecticut conforms to federal treatment. Keep detailed receipts and business purpose notes for each trip.

$

Client Meetings & Local Meals

Meals with clients or assistants during local business meetings are 50% deductible. Connecticut follows the federal 50% limit. Record the purpose and attendees. Meals must not be extravagant and must be directly tied to business activities.

$

Parking, Tolls & Local Transport

Business-related parking fees, tolls, ride shares, or public transit fares are deductible. These expenses reduce federal income and flow through to Connecticut without adjustment. Save receipts and record trip details for substantiation.

$

Vehicle Depreciation & Lease Payments

If you lease a car or deduct depreciation, follow federal guidelines for caps and Section 179 limits. Connecticut matches federal depreciation rules. Maintain purchase or lease agreements and depreciation logs as needed.

Contracted Labor & Assistants
-----------------------------

Deductions for contracted labor

Contract Labor (Second Shooters, Editors, Stylists)

Payments to freelancers like second shooters, editors, and stylists are fully deductible. Issue Form 1099-NEC if you pay an individual over $600/year. Connecticut follows federal rules—no separate filing or treatment required at the state level.

$

Assistant & Day-Rate Hires

Occasional assistants hired for setup, lighting, or production are considered contractors if not under your direct control. Payments are deductible. Connecticut does not impose withholding requirements on freelancers unless they are misclassified employees.

$

Professional Services (Makeup Artists, Hair Stylists)

Hiring stylists or makeup artists for client shoots is a deductible business expense. Maintain invoices and document the shoot purpose. Connecticut accepts all legitimate federal deductions—no adjustment needed on the state return.

$

Recordkeeping & W-9 Collection

Collect a W-9 form from all contractors and keep invoices or payment records. Connecticut does not require you to send a state copy of 1099-NEC unless backup withholding is involved.

$

Employee Classification Caution

If you control a worker’s schedule, provide equipment, or set strict rules, they may be considered employees. Misclassification can trigger penalties. Connecticut follows federal classification rules and audits for enforcement.

$

Conferences & Industry Events

Attending photography expos, conventions, or business summits qualifies as a write-off. Travel, lodging, and entry fees are deductible. Connecticut follows federal rules, so these deductions flow through without state-level adjustment.

$

Books, Subscriptions & Online Learning

Educational books, trade magazines, and digital subscriptions (like photography journals or editing tutorials) are deductible. Connecticut does not require any special treatment—these federal deductions reduce your Connecticut income.

$

Certification Fees & Exam Costs

Costs for certification programs (e.g., CPP exam) or renewal fees are deductible if related to your business. Connecticut accepts these as ordinary business expenses with no state-specific rules.

$

Documentation & Relevance

Deductions for marketing & advertising

Advertising & Promotions (Print, Digital, Social)

Costs for ads—online (Google, Meta), print, flyers, brochures, wedding directories—are fully deductible. This includes design, production, and placement. Connecticut conforms to federal rules, so no state adjustment is required.

$

Website Design, Hosting & Maintenance

Expenses for domain registration, website hosting, portfolio templates, design services, and plugin purchases are deductible. These are treated as software or advertising costs. CT follows federal rules—no special caps or reporting.

$

Client Outreach Tools & Email Marketing

Services like Mailchimp, CRM platforms, or booking forms that support lead generation are deductible. Subscription fees or app licenses used for client acquisition reduce federal and CT taxable income.

$

Promotional Events & Branded Materials

Booth fees at expos, costs for swag, branded merchandise, or print portfolios are marketing write-offs. Connecticut allows full deduction under ordinary business expense treatment—no additional state credit or cap applies.

Sales Tax on Marketing Materials

If printed materials (like brochures or postcards) are produced in Connecticut, sales tax may apply. The tax paid is part of the deductible cost. Online ad services and digital designs are generally tax-exempt in CT.

Deductions for insurance & licenses

Business Insurance Premiums

Premiums for general liability, equipment, property, and E&O policies are fully deductible. Connecticut follows federal rules—there’s no disallowance or adjustment. Save policy invoices showing business purpose and effective dates.

$

Home-Based Business or Studio Coverage

$

Business License & Registration Fees

$

Permits for Shoots (City, Park, or Venue)

Permit fees to photograph in state parks, city streets, or commercial venues are deductible. Connecticut treats them as standard costs—no state-level restrictions. Keep payment proof and location usage notes.

Legal & Professional Service Fees

Props, Backdrops & Consumable Supplies (Business Use)
-----------------------------------------------------

Deductions for supplies & consumables

Props, Backdrops & Studio Supplies

Items like backdrops, décor, client wardrobe, props, makeup, and other shoot-specific supplies are fully deductible. Connecticut follows federal guidelines—no addbacks required. Keep receipts and separate personal-use items from business purchases.

$

Consumables & Minor Gear

Small gear (e.g., batteries, cables, SD cards, light bulbs, lens wipes) under $2,500 can be expensed under the de minimis rule. CT follows federal rules; no state-level limitation applies. Maintain receipts for accurate deductions.

$

Printing & Album Fulfillment Costs

Printing labs, album production, custom packaging, and delivery (mailing USBs or prints) are deductible. Connecticut includes these in business expense calculations. Sales tax on purchases is part of the cost—track all fulfillment expenses.

$

Inventory vs. Supply Classification

If you stock and resell items like frames or albums, deduct them as inventory. Most photographers can expense these under cash-basis rules. Connecticut follows federal small business inventory treatment—no state-specific threshold adjustment.

$

Sales Tax on Props & Deliverables

CT sales tax (6.35%) applies to items you buy and resell. Use a resale certificate to avoid tax on qualifying resale items. For props used during service and not resold, pay the sales tax and deduct the full cost.

Deductions for financial expenses

Business Bank & Merchant Fees

Monthly account fees, check orders, ATM charges, and payment processor fees (PayPal, Stripe, Square) are fully deductible. CT follows federal treatment. Retain statements and processing records; no state-level disallowance or adjustment applies.

$

Business Loan & Credit Card Interest

Interest on loans, equipment financing, and business credit cards is deductible. Only the interest portion qualifies—not principal. Connecticut conforms to federal rules for business interest deductions. Keep lender and credit card statements for backup.

$

Payment Platform & Transaction Charges

Fees from online payment platforms or financial apps (e.g., Venmo for Business, QuickBooks Payments) are deductible. These reduce net income. CT has no state-specific carveouts—federal deductions flow through on your CT return.

$

Credit Card Annual Fees & Foreign Charges

Annual fees on business-use cards and foreign transaction fees tied to business expenses are deductible. CT allows full deduction if recorded on your federal return. Allocate amounts proportionally if using a personal card for business.

$

Financial & Tax Professional Fees

Fees paid to accountants, bookkeepers, or tax professionals for business-related services are deductible. CT accepts federal treatment. Personal tax prep fees (e.g., Form 1040) are not deductible, but Schedule C-related costs are.
