What Is Invoice Financing?
--------------------------

Invoice financing is a type of short-term borrowing where a business uses its unpaid customer invoices as collateral to get immediate funding. Instead of waiting 30, 60, or even 90 days to receive payment, you can get a large percentage (usually 70–90%) of the invoice amount upfront from a lender.

‍

In simple terms: You sell your unpaid invoice to a lender who gives you most of the money now, and the rest (minus fees) when your client pays.

How Does Invoice Financing Work?
--------------------------------

The Basic Structure

Here’s how a typical invoice financing process flows:

*   You deliver goods or services to a client.
*   You send them an invoice, usually with net-30 or net-60 [payment terms](https://joinotto.com/invoicing/payment-terms).
*   You approach an invoice financing company or platform.
*   They advance you a percentage (say, 85%) of the invoice amount.
*   Once your client pays the full invoice, you receive the remaining 15%, minus any fees.

‍

**_Example:_**_You invoice a client for $10,000. The financing company gives you $8,500 now. When your client pays the $10,000, you get back $1,500 minus, say, a $300 fee. You keep $1,200._

Types of Invoice Financing
--------------------------

There are two main forms of invoice financing:

1. Invoice Factoring

*   The lender buys the invoice and collects payment from the customer directly.
*   Your client is aware of the arrangement.
*   Easier approval, but may affect client relationships.

2. Invoice Discounting

*   You retain ownership of the invoice.
*   You collect from your client as usual.
*   Your customer likely doesn’t know you’re using financing.

Why Businesses Use Invoice Financing
------------------------------------

Solving [Cash Flow](https://joinotto.com/cash-flow) Gaps

*   Invoice financing is especially useful for businesses that:
*   Have long payment cycles
*   Offer credit terms to customers
*   Experience seasonal income
*   Need immediate capital for operations or growth

‍

Key Benefits

*   Fast access to cash
*   No need for perfect credit (since invoices act as collateral)
*   Preserves equity (unlike giving up shares to investors)
*   Scales with sales (more invoices = more potential funding)

**_Real-Life Story: Meet Alex, a Creative Agency Owner_**

Alex runs a boutique branding agency. His clients include large corporations who love his work—but they pay on net-60 terms. Meanwhile, Alex needs to pay staff, cover rent, and invest in new tools.

By using invoice financing, Alex unlocks 85% of invoice value upfront. Now, he doesn’t have to delay payroll or stress about overhead. When the client finally pays, Alex settles the remaining balance with the lender. It’s not free money—but it’s predictable cash flow.

Risks and Downsides to Consider
-------------------------------

It’s Not Free

Invoice financing comes with fees, typically 1–3% of the invoice amount per month. If your clients take too long to pay, the costs add up fast.

It Might Signal Cash Flow Issues

Depending on the model, clients may be alerted that you’re using invoice financing. In some industries, this could signal cash trouble—rightly or wrongly.

You’re Still Responsible if the Client Doesn’t Pay

With recourse financing, you are liable if your client defaults. Non-recourse options exist but often come at a higher cost.

When Should You Use Invoice Financing?
--------------------------------------

Consider invoice financing if:

*   You have predictable, reliable clients
*   You need a short-term liquidity boost
*   You don’t want to take on debt or dilute ownership
*   You’re in a growth phase but need upfront cash

Avoid it if:

*   Your profit margins are thin
*   Your clients are slow or inconsistent payers
*   You can’t afford the associated fees
